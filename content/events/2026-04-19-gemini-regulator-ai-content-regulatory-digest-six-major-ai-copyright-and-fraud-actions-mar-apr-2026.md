## Implication

Together, these actions show a hardening regulatory consensus that AI training on copyrighted data requires licensing and transparency, not broad exemptions. The UK's reversal—abandoning the TDM opt-out in favor of market-led licensing—removes what had been a potential laissez-faire pathway and aligns with the EU Parliament's mandatory licensing position. The EC's intervention in Denmark and the Court of Rome's annulment of the OpenAI fine signal fragmentation risk: member states are legislating independently, and litigation is unpredictable. For AI developers and content platforms, this phase closes the window for uncompensated training; licensing infrastructure must now develop rapidly to avoid regulatory escalation in both the EU and potential US consumer-protection enforcement. Publishers and creators benefit from stronger compensation norms, but the lack of a unified EU-wide licensing framework (vs. patchwork national laws) creates operational friction and legal uncertainty for cross-border AI services.

## What changed

[The European Parliament adopted a resolution (March 10, 2026) requiring EU copyright law to apply to all generative AI systems sold in the EU market, regardless of training location, and calling for fair remuneration, transparency, and a new licensing market.](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/doceo/document/TA-10-2026-0066_EN.html) [The UK government reversed its prior position and announced a "wait and see" approach rather than adopting a broad text-and-data-mining exception with opt-out protection (March 18, 2026)](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/report-and-impact-assessment-on-copyright-and-artificial-intelligence/report-on-copyright-and-artificial-intelligence), signaling preference for industry-led licensing. [The European Commission formally challenged Denmark's draft law adding copyright protections for AI-generated imitations of individuals' likeness and artistic performances (February 2, 2026).](https://www.crossborderdataforum.org/generative-ai-and-gdpr-enforcement-in-europe-a-lot-of-noise-one-fine-zero-survivors/) [The Court of Rome annulled Italy's €15 million GDPR fine against OpenAI (March 18, 2026), though the Garante may appeal.](https://www.crossborderdataforum.org/generative-ai-and-gdpr-enforcement-in-europe-a-lot-of-noise-one-fine-zero-survivors/) [The US Congress is advancing the "AI Fraud Accountability Act" to treat AI voice fraud as an FTC unfair-or-deceptive-practices violation (April 17, 2026).](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202604/ai-voice-fraud-draws-new-congressional-scrutiny)
