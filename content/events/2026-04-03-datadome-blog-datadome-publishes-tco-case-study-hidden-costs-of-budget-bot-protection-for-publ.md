## What changed

A new long-form blog post appeared in DataDome's feed presenting a detailed total-cost-of-ownership (TCO) case study for a publisher that had ~100 million monthly page views. The post quantifies three hidden cost centers for a patchwork bot-protection stack: CAPTCHA licensing (~$1,608/month), engineering labor for manual mitigation (~$3,048/month from three engineers spending 3–4 hours/week each), and infrastructure waste from bot traffic (~$1,620/month). A summary table puts the publisher's pre-DataDome quantified cost at $6,276/month ($75,312/year) versus DataDome's managed offering. The piece also introduces "intent-based detection" and "agentic commerce" as framing concepts, signaling DataDome's positioning around AI-agent traffic differentiation.

## Implication

For publishers and media operators, the post offers a concrete cost-accounting framework they can apply internally to audit CAPTCHA fees, engineering overhead, and bot-inflated cloud bills—useful even if the specific numbers are illustrative rather than audited. The emphasis on distinguishing "good AI agents" (agentic commerce, SEO crawlers) from malicious bots reflects a broader industry shift: bot-management vendors are repositioning to address AI-agent traffic, which is relevant to any operator thinking about how to selectively allow LLM or commerce agents. The TCO framing is a sales narrative, but the labor-cost and infrastructure-waste methodology is a repeatable model for practitioners building internal business cases for bot-protection investment. Publishers with ad-supported business models should note the explicit call-out of bot-skewed analytics and ad fraud as compounding risks beyond infrastructure spend.
