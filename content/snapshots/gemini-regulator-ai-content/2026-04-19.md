Here is a compact digest of the most important distinct regulatory actions and developments concerning AI training data, AI crawlers, content licensing, or generative AI and copyright from the last 30 days (March 20, 2026 - April 19, 2026):

1.  **European Parliament Adopts Resolution on AI and Copyright**
    On March 10, 2026, the European Parliament adopted a resolution outlining its position on copyright and generative artificial intelligence. The resolution calls for EU copyright law to apply to all generative AI systems available in the EU market, regardless of where the training takes place, and emphasizes the need for fair remuneration for copyrighted material used in AI training, full transparency from AI providers regarding training data, and the establishment of a new licensing market. It also asserts that content entirely generated by AI should not be protected by copyright.

2.  **UK Government Shifts Stance on AI Copyright Exception**
    On March 18, 2026, the UK government published its Report on Copyright and Artificial Intelligence, announcing a significant change in its approach. It confirmed that it no longer favors a broad exception to copyright infringement for text and data mining (TDM) with an opt-out model for rights owners. Instead, the government will adopt a "wait and see" approach, allowing industry-led licensing arrangements to develop and monitoring global legal developments before considering legislative changes.

3.  **European Commission Questions Denmark's Draft AI Copyright Law**
    On February 2, 2026, the European Commission sent a formal letter to Denmark, raising concerns about its draft law amending the national Copyright Act to introduce new protections against the unauthorized use of digitally generated imitations of individuals' personal characteristics and artistic performances by AI. The Commission questioned the compatibility of these proposed measures with existing EU copyright rules and the broader legal framework governing intellectual property.

4.  **Court of Rome Annuls Italian Garante's Fine Against OpenAI**
    On March 18, 2026, the Court of Rome annulled the Italian Data Protection Authority's (Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali) November 2, 2024 decision to impose a €15 million fine on OpenAI for multiple GDPR violations related to its ChatGPT service. The Garante is currently assessing whether to lodge an appeal against this ruling.

5.  **UK Communications and Digital Committee Calls for Stronger Copyright Enforcement for AI**
    On March 6, 2026, the UK Communications and Digital Committee published its report "AI, copyright and the creative industries," concluding that the UK's copyright framework is not outdated but is threatened by widespread unlicensed use of protected works and limited transparency from AI developers. The report urged the government to support a licensing-based AI development model and to rule out a new commercial text and data mining exception with an opt-out.

6.  **Proposed US "AI Fraud Accountability Act" Targets AI Voice Fraud via FTC**
    On April 17, 2026, news reports indicated that the proposed "AI Fraud Accountability Act" would create a civil and regulatory enforcement route through the US Federal Trade Commission (FTC) for AI voice fraud. If enacted, a violation would be treated as an unfair or deceptive act or practice enforceable by the FTC, aiming to make digital impersonation fraud a matter of consumer protection enforcement.
