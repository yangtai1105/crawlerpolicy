Here is a compact digest of important regulatory developments from the last 30 days concerning AI training data, AI crawlers, content licensing, and generative AI and copyright:

1.  **European Parliament Adopts Resolution on Copyright and Generative AI**
    On March 10, 2026, the European Parliament adopted a resolution on "Copyright and Generative Artificial Intelligence – Opportunities and Challenges." The resolution calls for strengthening the EU copyright framework to ensure creators retain control over their works used for AI training and receive fair remuneration. It also proposes measures to increase transparency regarding copyright-protected works used in AI training and supports the establishment of collective licensing frameworks. The resolution emphasizes that EU copyright law should apply to generative AI models available in the EU market, regardless of where the training takes place.

2.  **UK Government Abandons Broad AI Copyright Exception for Text and Data Mining**
    In March and April 2026, the UK government confirmed it is no longer pursuing a broad exception to copyright infringement for text and data mining (TDM) with an opt-out for rights owners. This shift in policy follows concerns from creative industries about allowing AI companies to use copyrighted material for training without compensation. The government will instead adopt a "wait and see" approach, encouraging industry-led licensing arrangements and monitoring global legal developments before considering legislative changes.

3.  **White House Releases National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence**
    On March 20, 2026, the White House published a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence, which includes recommendations for Congress on intellectual property rights. The framework suggests considering licensing frameworks or collective rights systems to allow creators to collectively negotiate compensation from AI providers. While the administration believes that training AI models on copyrighted material *currently* does not violate copyright laws, it supports allowing courts to resolve this question and recommends against congressional action that would affect judicial fair use determinations. The framework also recommends establishing federal protections against the unauthorized distribution or commercial use of AI-generated digital replicas of an individual's voice, likeness, or other identifiable attributes.

4.  **Italian Garante's €15 Million Fine Against OpenAI Annulled by Court**
    On March 18, 2026, the Court of Rome annulled the Italian Data Protection Authority's (Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali) €15 million fine against OpenAI for multiple GDPR violations related to its ChatGPT service. The Garante had imposed the fine in November 2024, citing issues such as processing personal data for training without an adequate legal basis, failing to inform users, and lacking age verification mechanisms. The full reasoning for the court's annulment has not yet been published, and the Garante is assessing whether to appeal.

5.  **UK CMA Intensifies Scrutiny of AI and Algorithms in Competition and Consumer Protection**
    The UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) signaled a significant intensification of its scrutiny of businesses' use of algorithms and AI in competition law and consumer protection, as detailed in its Annual Plan for 2026-2027 and new guidance published around April 10, 2026. The CMA identified four categories of algorithmic collusion risk and confirmed that businesses bear full responsibility for the consumer-facing conduct of their AI agents. This includes ensuring AI agents respect statutory rights, avoid misleading customers, and obtain necessary consents, with businesses expected to train and test AI agents thoroughly.
