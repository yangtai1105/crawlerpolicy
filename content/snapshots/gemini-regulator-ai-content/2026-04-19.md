Here is a compact digest of recent regulatory actions concerning AI training data, AI crawlers, content licensing, and generative AI and copyright from the last 30 days:

1.  **UK Government Shifts Stance on AI Training and Copyright Exception**
    The UK government has withdrawn its proposal for a broad copyright exception that would have allowed AI companies to use copyrighted material for training by default, with an opt-out for rights holders. This decision, outlined in a report published on March 18, 2026, follows extensive engagement with creative industries and other stakeholders. The government now intends to support industry-led licensing solutions and monitor global developments before considering legislative changes.

2.  **European Parliament Adopts Resolution on Copyright and Generative AI**
    On March 10, 2026, the European Parliament adopted a Resolution on Copyright and Generative Artificial Intelligence. The resolution advocates for a stronger EU copyright framework to ensure creators maintain control over their works used in AI training and receive fair remuneration. It also calls for increased transparency regarding copyrighted works used for AI training and proposes that EU copyright law should apply to generative AI models available in the EU, irrespective of where the training occurred.

3.  **Italian Court Annuls Garante's Fine Against OpenAI**
    The Court of Rome, on March 18, 2026, annulled a €15 million fine previously imposed by the Italian data protection authority (Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali) on OpenAI. The fine, issued in November 2024, was for alleged GDPR violations related to the processing of personal data for training its ChatGPT service without a sufficient legal basis. The full reasoning behind the court's decision has not yet been publicly released.
