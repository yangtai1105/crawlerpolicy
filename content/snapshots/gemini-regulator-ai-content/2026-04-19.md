Here's a compact digest of the most important distinct regulatory actions concerning AI training data, AI crawlers, content licensing, or generative AI and copyright from the last 30 days (March 20, 2026 - April 19, 2026):

1.  **European Parliament Adopts Resolution on Copyright and Generative AI**
    On March 10, 2026, the European Parliament adopted a Resolution on Copyright and Generative Artificial Intelligence, signaling a potential shift in European copyright policy. The resolution, which builds on a report from the Parliament's Committee on Legal Affairs, suggests that traditional copyright rules may not adequately address the realities of generative AI training and calls for a broader territorial reach of EU copyright law for AI systems offered in the European market, regardless of where the training takes place. It also emphasizes the need for transparency regarding copyright-protected works used for AI training and supports a collective licensing framework to ensure fair remuneration for creators.
    Source: European Parliament (as referenced by multiple legal analyses)

2.  **UK Government Steps Back from Broad AI Copyright Exception**
    The UK government published its Report and Economic Impact Assessment on the use of copyright works in the development of AI systems on March 18, 2026. The report confirms that the government is no longer pursuing a broad exception to copyright infringement for text and data mining (TDM) with an opt-out for rights owners. Instead, the UK is adopting a "wait and see" approach, intending to allow industry-led licensing arrangements to develop and to monitor global legal developments before considering legislative changes.
    Source: GOV.UK (Report on Copyright and Artificial Intelligence)

3.  **Italian Court Annuls Garante's Fine Against OpenAI**
    On March 18, 2026, the Court of Rome annulled a €15 million fine that the Italian data protection authority (Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali) had imposed on OpenAI on November 2, 2024. The fine was related to multiple GDPR violations concerning the management of its ChatGPT service, including the processing of personal data for training without an adequate legal basis. The full reasoning for the annulment has not yet been published, and the Garante is assessing whether to appeal.
    Source: Dentons (referencing the Garante's actions and the court's decision)
