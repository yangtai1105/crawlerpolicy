1.  **European Parliament Adopts Recommendations on Copyright and Generative AI**
    On March 10, 2026, the European Parliament adopted a series of recommendations aimed at protecting copyrighted creative work from use by artificial intelligence. The recommendations emphasize the need for transparency and fair remuneration for the use of copyrighted material by generative AI, proposing a new licensing market and an opt-out mechanism for rights holders. MEPs also stated that content fully generated by AI should not be protected by copyright.

2.  **UK Government Shifts Stance on AI Copyright Exception, Adopts "Wait and See" Approach**
    The UK government has published its Report and Economic Impact Assessment on the use of copyright works in AI systems, confirming a move away from its previously preferred option of a broad exception to copyright infringement for text and data mining (TDM) with an opt-out for rights owners. Instead, the government will now allow industry-led licensing arrangements to develop, monitor global legal developments, and further engage with stakeholders before considering legislative changes. This approach was outlined in a report by the Communications and Digital Committee on March 6, 2026, and further detailed in a government statement and report around April 8, 2026.

    On March 18, 2026, the Court of Rome annulled the €15 million fine that the Italian data protection authority (Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali) had imposed on OpenAI.
